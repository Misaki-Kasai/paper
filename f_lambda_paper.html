Structural Note on the Variational Section

This model intentionally adopts a quasi-solution \(\phi(r)\), rather than an exact solution. The reason is that an exact solution would eliminate the physical significance of the characteristic scale \(r = R\), thereby preventing the reproduction of the essential behavior: scale-responsive structure of space.

If an exact solution were enforced, the parameter \(r = R\) would likely be absorbed into coordinate redefinitions or symmetry-induced degeneracies. In such a case, \(R\) would lose its status as a physically anchored scale and instead become a redundant parameter, thereby obscuring any meaningful scale-dependent structure in the resulting dynamics.

Being a quasi-solution is not a weakness but the structural foundation of the model—it is the key to realizing scale-dependent spatial dynamics.

To verify the validity of this quasi-solution, we conducted a variational residual analysis. The results show that the deviation is well-controlled, localized, and physically meaningful. This behavior is also visually illustrated in Fig. 1.

Please do not mistake the use of a quasi-solution for a flaw or a compromise. It is not a shortcoming, but the core of the theory and the very foundation of its structure.

Importantly, the quasi-solution is not adopted for approximation, but for its structural role in deriving the effective cosmological term \(\Lambda_{\mathrm{eff}}(r)\).

It does not imply that the entire structure of the universe or the fundamental field equations themselves are quasi-solutions. The aim of this study is to examine whether a quasi-structural term like \(\Lambda_{\mathrm{eff}}(r)\) can function effectively within a universe that is otherwise governed by exact solutions.

This quasi-solution structure has been confirmed to bridge both the cosmological scale (~\(10^{26}\,\text{m}\)) and the Higgs scale (~\(10^{-18}\,\text{m}\)). See also the Appendix for numerical estimates related to the Higgs scale, as well as a discussion of the conservation laws within this theory.

Abstract

In this study, we propose an extension of the Einstein field equations by introducing a repulsive gravity framework based on the scale-responsive geometric repulsion effect of space, derived from a scalar field. This effect is modeled as a logarithmic-type potential structure, from which a scale-dependent effective cosmological term, \(\Lambda_{\text{eff}}\), is derived. The impact of \(\Lambda_{\text{eff}}\) depends on spatial scale, potentially redefining the structural properties of gravity especially at galactic scales and within black hole interiors. We demonstrate that two key observational and theoretical challenges—the flattening of galactic rotation curves in their outer regions and the avoidance of black hole singularities—can be addressed in a unified and quantitative manner through the common scalar-field-derived term \(\Lambda_{\text{eff}}\).

Overview of the Galactic Rotation Curve Problem and the Singularity Problem

Recent astronomical observations have revealed that the rotational velocities of stars in the outer regions of galaxies significantly exceed the values predicted by gravitational theory (general relativity) combined with the observed amount of baryonic matter. This discrepancy is known as the galactic rotation curve problem. It has long been considered difficult to account for this phenomenon without invoking the existence of dark matter [Rubin, V. C., & Ford, W. K. (1980). Rotation of the Andromeda Nebula from a Spectroscopic Survey of Emission Regions. The Astrophysical Journal]; [Sofue, Y., & Rubin, V. (2001). Rotation Curves of Spiral Galaxies. Annual Review of Astronomy and Astrophysics].

On the other hand, gravitational collapse inside black holes is known, in the classical framework, to lead to a singularity—i.e., a region of infinite curvature—in the limit \(r \to 0\). This phenomenon is considered to signal the breakdown or limitation of general relativity itself [Penrose, R. (1965). Gravitational Collapse and Space-Time Singularities. Physical Review Letters]. The so-called singularity problem strongly suggests the necessity of quantum corrections or structural modifications within gravitational theory.

Successes and Limitations of the \(\Lambda\)CDM Model

The \(\Lambda\)CDM model, which serves as the standard framework in modern cosmology, has achieved remarkable success in explaining observational data such as the cosmic microwave background (CMB), baryon acoustic oscillations (BAO), and Type Ia supernovae with high precision [Planck Collaboration (2020). Planck 2018 Results. VI. Cosmological Parameters. Astronomy & Astrophysics]. However, the cosmological constant \(\Lambda\) is defined as a static and uniform quantity, without incorporating any spatial structure or scale dependence.

Moreover, although \(\Lambda\)CDM relies on invisible components such as dark matter and dark energy, the true nature of these entities remains unknown. They function as auxiliary terms in the theoretical framework, and there has been persistent criticism that they lack a solid basis for physical reality [Padmanabhan, T. (2003). Cosmological Constant—The Weight of the Vacuum. Physics Reports].

Motivation: Constructing a Cosmological Framework with Repulsion as the Subject

This study attempts to construct a gravitational theory in which repulsive response is the subject, in contrast to general relativity, which treats “gravity = attraction” as its primary perspective.

Specifically, we propose a framework that introduces an effective cosmological constant term, \(\Lambda_{\text{eff}}\), derived from a scalar field, which acts as a scale-dependent repulsive potential. This enables a unified description of gravitational structures ranging from galactic scales to black hole singularity scales.

It should be noted that this study adopts the stance of preserving the cosmological constant \(\Lambda\) in the Einstein field equations as a true constant. However, its effect manifests in a scale-dependent manner through a logarithmic-type potential, and is therefore reflected in the observable gravitational structure as \(\Lambda_{\text{eff}}(r)\). In this way, the aim is to construct a gravitational theory that retains the philosophical foundation of the \(\Lambda\)CDM model while overcoming its structural limitations.

Structure of the Effective Term \(\Lambda_{\text{eff}}\) and Derivation from the Scalar Field Potential

In this section, we demonstrate that the effective \(\Lambda\) term

\[ \Lambda_{\text{eff}}(r) = \frac{4r^2}{(R^2 + r^2)^2} \]

is structurally derived from a variational principle applied to a spherically symmetric and static scalar field.

Here, \(R\) is a characteristic scale of space and corresponds to the radial position at which the effective term \(\Lambda_{\text{eff}}(r)\) reaches its maximum. It serves as a physically meaningful parameter in this framework.

The scalar field \(\phi\) considered in this study is rigorously defined based on a Lorentz-invariant Lagrangian density. In its general form, this Lagrangian is given by

\[ \mathcal{L}_\phi = -\frac{1}{2} g^{\mu\nu} \partial_\mu \phi \, \partial_\nu \phi - V(\phi), \]

where \(V(\phi)\) is the scalar potential. However, restricting ourselves to a spherically symmetric and static configuration \(\phi = \phi(r)\), we simplify the Lagrangian to the form

\[ \mathcal{L}_\phi = -\frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{d\phi}{dr} \right)^2 - V(\phi). \]

This reduction is justified as an effective theory based on the symmetry of the solution and does not imply a violation of the original Lorentz invariance of the full Lagrangian.

In this context, the isotropic structure of the corresponding energy-momentum tensor is given by:

\[ T_{\mu\nu}^{(\phi)} = \left[ \frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{d\phi}{dr} \right)^2 + V(\phi) \right] g_{\mu\nu}. \]

We now assume the following form for the effective cosmological term, which depends on spatial scale:

\[ \Lambda_{\text{eff}}(r) = \frac{4r^2}{(R^2 + r^2)^2}. \]

A natural choice of scalar field that reproduces this structure is:

\[ \phi(r) = \frac{2r}{R^2 + r^2}. \]

Using this expression for \(\phi(r)\), the corresponding potential \(V(\phi(r))\) expands as follows:

\[ \phi(r)^2 = \left( \frac{2r}{R^2 + r^2} \right)^2 = \frac{4r^2}{(R^2 + r^2)^2}, \] \[ V(\phi(r)) = -\frac{1}{4} \cdot \phi^2 \left( 4 - R^2 \phi^2 \right) = -\frac{1}{4} \cdot \frac{4r^2}{(R^2 + r^2)^2} \left( 4 - \frac{4R^2 r^2}{(R^2 + r^2)^2} \right), \] \[ = -\frac{4r^2}{(R^2 + r^2)^2} + \underbrace{\frac{4R^2 r^4}{(R^2 + r^2)^4}}_{\text{Second-order correction term}}. \]

Here, the second term serves as a correction that slightly modifies the primary structure of \(\Lambda_{\text{eff}}\), but the leading term clearly satisfies the relation:

\[ \boxed{V(\phi(r)) \propto -\Lambda_{\text{eff}}(r)}. \]

In particular, in the regime \(r \ll R\), the correction term is significantly smaller than the leading term. In the opposite limit \(r \gg R\), we observe the following asymptotic behaviors:

\[ \frac{r^2}{(R^2 + r^2)^2} \approx \frac{1}{r^2}, \quad \frac{r^4}{(R^2 + r^2)^4} \approx \frac{1}{r^4}, \]

indicating that the contribution from the correction term decays rapidly.

Furthermore, even at the intermediate scale \(r = R\), we find:

\[ \frac{4R^2}{(2R^2)^2} = \frac{1}{R^2}, \quad \frac{4R^2 R^4}{(2R^2)^4} = \frac{1}{4R^2}, \]

showing that the correction term remains only one-fourth of the leading term.

Therefore, across all spatial scales, the leading term dominates, and it is demonstrated that \(\Lambda_{\text{eff}}(r)\) can be directly and naturally derived from the scalar field potential \(V(\phi)\).

Variational Evaluation of the Scalar Field

In this section, we evaluate whether the Lagrangian of the scalar field \(\phi(r)\), derived from the effective cosmological term \(\Lambda_{\text{eff}}(r)\) constructed in Section 4, satisfies the extremum condition based on the variational principle (Euler–Lagrange equation). In particular, we expect that the extremum condition is locally violated around the spatial scale \(r = R\), and that this violation rapidly diminishes for \(r \gg R\).

Such behavior is naturally anticipated from the structure of the adopted \(\phi(r)\), and it also corresponds to the notion that cosmic acceleration may be closely related to a local violation of the extremum condition.

Note that the violation of the extremum condition discussed here does not directly imply a violation of the conservation law \(\nabla^\mu T_{\mu\nu} = 0\).

Euler–Lagrange Equation and Variational Residual

In Section 4, the following scalar field was adopted as a natural candidate for reproducing the spatial dependence of the effective cosmological constant, based on the structure of the scalar potential \( V(\phi) \):

\[ \phi(r) = \frac{2r}{R^2 + r^2} \]

The corresponding potential takes the following form:

\[ V(\phi) = -\frac{1}{4} \phi^2 \left( 4 - R^2 \phi^2 \right) \]

Under the assumption of spherical symmetry, the Lagrangian density becomes:

\[ \mathcal{L}_\phi = -\frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{d\phi}{dr} \right)^2 - V(\phi) = -\frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{d\phi}{dr} \right)^2 + \frac{1}{4} \phi^2 \left( 4 - R^2 \phi^2 \right) \]

The Euler–Lagrange equation derived from this Lagrangian is:

\[ \frac{d^2\phi}{dr^2} = R^2 \phi^3 - 2\phi \]

Here, in order to evaluate how well the assumed scalar field \(\phi(r)\) satisfies the extremum condition, we conduct the analysis under the following boundary conditions:

\[ \phi(0) = 0, \quad \phi'(0) = \frac{2}{R^2} \]

with the characteristic scale \(R = 2\), which is chosen because it highlights the features of the scalar field structure and the behavior of the effective \(\Lambda\) term most clearly, while also ensuring clarity in the analytical results and numerical plots.

Under these conditions, the deviation from the extremum condition (the variational residual) is defined as follows:

\[ \delta(r) \equiv \frac{d^2\phi}{dr^2} - \left( R^2 \phi^3 - 2\phi \right) \]

By substituting the assumed scalar field \(\phi(r)\) into this expression, the variational residual is explicitly given by:

\[ \delta(r) = \left[ \frac{4r(r^2 - 3R^2)}{(R^2 + r^2)^3} \right] - \left[ \frac{8R^2 r^3}{(R^2 + r^2)^3} - \frac{4r}{R^2 + r^2} \right] \]

Based on this expression, the variational residual \(\delta(r)\) for the scalar field \(\phi(r)\) was numerically evaluated. The resulting plots, including a close-up view, are shown in Fig. 1.

From Fig. 1, it can be seen that the variational residual \(\delta(r)\) begins to locally deviate at the spatial scale \(r = R\), reaches a peak shortly thereafter (\( r > R \)), and then rapidly decays. This result indicates that the scalar field specifically recognizes and responds to \(r = R\), and that the violation of the extremum condition is local and non-instantaneous. Such behavior is also consistent with the nature of the sustained cosmic acceleration.

The range of analysis is set to \( r \in [0.0001, 100] \). Within this interval, the maximum value of the variational residual \(\delta(r)\) is found to be:

\[ \delta_{\text{max}} \approx 0.552 \quad \text{at} \quad r \approx 4.10 \approx R \]

To evaluate the significance of this residual, we define the relative error with respect to the extremum condition as:

\[ \epsilon_\phi(r) \equiv \left| \frac{\delta(r)}{\phi(r)} \right| \]

At the point where the residual peaks (\(r \approx 4.10\)), the scalar field takes the value \(\phi(r) \approx 0.394\), yielding:

\[ \epsilon_\phi \approx 1.40 \]

Similarly, at the characteristic scale \(r = R = 2.00\), the relative error is:

\[ \epsilon_\phi(R) = \left| \frac{\delta(R)}{\phi(R)} \right| = 0.750 \quad (\delta(R) = 0.375,\ \phi(R) = 0.500) \]

Even around the characteristic scale \( r = R \), the relative error remains within a range that can be regarded as a local violation of the extremum condition.

The value \( \epsilon_\phi(R) = 0.750 \) supports the validity of the function as a quasi-solution.

On the other hand, the variational residual \( \delta(r) \) reaches its maximum around \( r \approx 4.10 \), where the relative error rises to approximately \( \epsilon_\phi \approx 1.40 \).

However, since \( \phi(r) \) becomes small at this scale, the relative error is numerically overestimated due to the small denominator.

Therefore, it is more appropriate to evaluate the validity of the quasi-solution primarily around \( r = R \).

Based on these considerations, the scalar field \( \phi(r) \) adopted in this study, while not an exact solution, can be regarded as a physically acceptable quasi-solution that does not significantly violate the variational principle.

In this paper, we refer to the structure based on the effective cosmological term \(\Lambda_{\text{eff}}(r)\), constructed from the quasi-solution \(\phi(r)\), as the \(\Lambda_{\text{eff}}\) model (or the f\(\Lambda\) theory).

Implications and the Extended Einstein Field Equations

The modified Einstein field equation corresponding to this effective energy density is written as:

\[ \Lambda_{\text{eff}}(r) g_{\mu\nu} + G_{\mu\nu} = \kappa T_{\mu\nu}^{(\text{matter})}, \] or, equivalently, with \(\Lambda_{\text{eff}}\) moved to the right-hand side: \[ G_{\mu\nu} = \kappa T_{\mu\nu}^{(\text{matter})} - \Lambda_{\text{eff}}(r) g_{\mu\nu}. \]

At local scales, such as within the solar system, the following condition holds:

\[ \frac{\Lambda_{\text{eff}}(r)}{\kappa T_{\mu\nu}} \ll 1, \]

which naturally reduces the modified equation to the standard Einstein field equation.

Therefore, the theory remains consistent with well-established observations that are precisely explained by general relativity, such as the perihelion precession of Mercury and the gravitational redshift.

Application to Galactic Rotation Curves and the Effective Energy of the \(\Lambda\) Term

(1) Gravitational Term: Realistic Approximation Model (Spherically Symmetric Uniform Density)

In the central region of a galaxy, the baryonic matter is relatively concentrated, allowing the use of a spherically symmetric and uniform density approximation. Under this assumption, the mass \(M(r)\) enclosed within a radius \(r\) is given by:

\[ \rho = \text{const} \quad \Rightarrow \quad M(r) = \frac{4}{3} \pi r^3 \rho. \]

From the gravitational potential generated by this mass distribution, the rotational velocity is derived as:

\[ v_{\text{grav}}(r) = \sqrt{\frac{G M(r)}{r}} = \sqrt{\frac{4}{3} \pi G \rho} \cdot r \quad \Rightarrow \quad v_{\text{grav}}(r) \propto r. \]

This result is consistent with the linear increase in rotational velocity observed in the inner regions of galactic rotation curves. Indeed, observations show that near the galactic center, the rotational velocity tends to follow \(v \propto r\), making this model a good approximation in the gravity-dominated region of the galactic core.

(2) Repulsive Term: Rotational Velocity from the \(\Lambda\) Term and logarithmic-type Extension

While the gravitational term from the uniform density model dominates in the central region of galaxies, explaining the flattened rotation curves in the outer regions requires a different dynamical contribution.

In the present model, we assume that a scale-dependent effective \(\Lambda\) term, derived from a scalar field associated with a logarithmic-type potential, produces a repulsive effect on space. This effect contributes to the squared rotational velocity in the following form:

\[ v_{\text{rep}}^2(r) = v_0^2 \cdot \frac{2r^2}{R^2 + r^2}, \]

where:

  • \(v_0\) is a characteristic velocity scale associated with the repulsive term,
  • which becomes dominant in the outer galactic region (\(r \gg R\)).

Based on observational data, we adopt a representative value of \(v_0 \approx 200\,\mathrm{km/s}\). Since this function contributes negligibly near the center and asymptotically approaches a constant value \(v_{\text{rep}} \to v_0\) as \(r \to \infty\), it naturally reproduces the observed flattening of galactic rotation curves.

(3) Rotational Velocity from Energy Composition

In this model, the gravitational and repulsive contributions to the rotational velocity are regarded as independent energy components. Therefore, the observed rotational velocity is expressed as a quadratic energy sum:

\[ v_{\text{total}}^2(r) = v_{\text{grav}}^2(r) + v_{\text{rep}}^2(r) \quad \Rightarrow \quad v_{\text{total}}(r) = \sqrt{v_{\text{grav}}^2(r) + v_{\text{rep}}^2(r)}. \]

This composite structure implies that:

  • In the inner region, \(v_{\text{grav}} \propto r\) dominates,
  • While in the outer region, \(v_{\text{rep}} \to v_0\) flattens the rotation curve.

This framework demonstrates that the essential features of galactic rotation curves can be reproduced without assuming the existence of dark matter, thereby supporting the validity of the effective \(\Lambda\) term, \(\Lambda_{\text{eff}}\).

(4) Deriving the Velocity v₀ ≈ 200 km/s from the Effective Energy of \(\Lambda_{\text{eff}}(r)\)

The characteristic velocity scale \( v_0 \) can be derived from the result of Section 6 and the energy density of space in the \( \Lambda \)CDM model, which is given by

\[ \rho_\Lambda = \frac{\Lambda c^2}{8\pi G} \approx 10^{-26} \, \text{kg/m}^3. \]

By integrating this density over a spherically symmetric region of radius \( R \approx 1000\,\text{kpc} \approx 3.1 \times 10^{22}\,\text{m} \), we obtain the effective mass: \[ M_\Lambda = \rho_\Lambda \cdot \frac{4}{3} \pi R^3 \approx 10^{42} \, \text{kg}. \]

If we convert the entire energy associated with this mass into kinetic energy, the result is:

\[ E = \frac{1}{2} M_\Lambda v_0^2 \quad \Rightarrow \quad v_0 \approx \sqrt{\frac{2E}{M_\Lambda}} \approx 200\,\text{km/s}. \]

Therefore, the characteristic velocity \( v_0 \approx 200\,\mathrm{km/s} \) is naturally derived within the framework of the \(\Lambda_{\text{eff}}\) model, and the corresponding energy \( E \) is consistent with the typical observational energy scale \( E \sim 10^{52}\,\mathrm{J} \). Considering that the total vacuum energy is given by \( E_\Lambda = M_\Lambda c^2 \approx 10^{59}\,\mathrm{J} \), the energy scale \( E \sim 10^{52}\,\mathrm{J} \) is sufficiently small in comparison, ensuring energetic consistency. These results quantitatively support the physical plausibility of the scale-dependent term \( \Lambda_{\text{eff}}(r) \), derived from the scalar field, as an effective cosmological repulsive component.

Avoidance Conditions for Black Hole Singularities

As a scale-dependent repulsive force derived from the quasi-solution scalar field \(\phi(r)\), we introduce the following acceleration term:

\[ a(r) = \frac{2c_0 r}{R^2 + r^2} \]

Here, \(c_0\) is a repulsive potential coefficient (unit: \(\mathrm{m^2/s^2}\)), representing the spatial energy scale of the repulsive field.

Assumption: Inside the black hole, the repulsive acceleration acts spherically symmetrically and is assumed to balance the gravitational acceleration just before the singularity is reached.

Balance Condition Between Repulsive and Gravitational Forces: Under this assumption, the balance condition is expressed as:

\[ \frac{GM}{r^2} = \frac{2c_0 r}{R^2 + r^2} \]

where \(M\) denotes the mass of the black hole.

Simplified Condition: By adopting \(R = r\) as the characteristic scale, the balance condition simplifies to:
(The reason for adopting \( r = R \) (\( R = r \)) is that the function \(a(r)\) reaches its maximum at \( r = R \).)

\[ \frac{GM}{r^2} = \frac{c_0}{r} \quad \Rightarrow \quad c_0 = \frac{GM}{r} \]

Critical Condition for Singularity Avoidance: We define the critical radius \(r_{\text{crit}}\) at which the repulsive energy equals the total mass-energy of the black hole.

The repulsive energy is given by:

\[ E_{\text{rep}} = M \cdot c_0 = \frac{GM^2}{r} \]

The mass-energy of the black hole is:

\[ E_{\text{BH}} = M c^2 \]

This model assumes that the repulsive response of space is induced by the black hole’s own mass-energy. Thus, the model can be treated as a closed system.

Under this assumption, the principle of energy conservation implies that the repulsive energy accumulated within the black hole cannot exceed the total mass-energy of the black hole.

Therefore, setting the two energies equal yields the critical radius at which collapse becomes energetically impossible:

\[ E_{\text{rep}} = E_{\text{BH}} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \frac{GM^2}{r_{\text{crit}}} = M c^2 \quad \Rightarrow \quad r_{\text{crit}} = \frac{GM}{c^2} \]

Interpretation: This result implies that the collapse of a black hole naturally halts at a finite scale, which is determined solely by the mass \(M\), gravitational constant \(G\), and the speed of light \(c\).

This critical radius exactly corresponds to half the Schwarzschild radius:

\[ r_{\text{crit}} = \frac{1}{2} r_s, \quad \text{where} \quad r_s = \frac{2GM}{c^2} \]

Thus, the singularity is autonomously avoided not by an external cutoff, but by the internal repulsive structure of space itself within the theory.

(Since the condition \(M c_0 = M c^2\) (\(E_{\text{rep}} = E_{\text{BH}}\)) is satisfied at the critical radius, it follows that \(c_0 = c^2\) at that point. Consequently, singularities are prohibited by the principle of energy conservation and the speed-of-light limit.)

Consistency with the Higgs Mechanism

In this section, we show that the vacuum expectation value and mass term derived from the potential

\[ V(\phi(r)) = -\frac{1}{4} \cdot \phi^2 \left( 4 - R^2 \phi^2 \right) = -\phi^2 + \frac{1}{4} R^2 \phi^4 \]

defined in Section 4, match those of the Higgs mechanism in the Standard Model.

In the Standard Model, the effective potential for the Higgs mechanism is given by:

\[ V_{\text{Higgs}}(\phi) = -\frac{1}{2} \mu^2 \phi^2 + \frac{1}{4} \lambda \phi^4 \]

The vacuum expectation value \( v \) in this structure is determined by the extremum condition:

\[ \left. \frac{dV}{d\phi} \right|_{\phi = v} = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad v = \sqrt{\frac{\mu^2}{\lambda}} \]

Vacuum Expectation Value

Differentiating the potential \( V(\phi(r)) \) in the \( \Lambda_{\text{eff}} \) model yields:

\[ \frac{dV}{d\phi} = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \phi(-2 + R^2 \phi^2) = 0 \]

Therefore, the solutions are:

\[ \phi = 0 \quad \text{or} \quad \phi = \pm \frac{\sqrt{2}}{R} \]

Thus, excluding the trivial solution \( \phi = 0 \), the vacuum expectation value in the \( \Lambda_{\text{eff}} \) model is:

\[ v = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{R}, \quad \phi = \pm v \]

Now, in order to demonstrate the consistency, we compare the coefficients of the quadratic and quartic terms between \( V(\phi(r)) \) and \( V_{\text{Higgs}}(\phi) \), and obtain:

\[ -\phi^2 \leftrightarrow -\frac{1}{2} \mu^2 \phi^2 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \mu^2 = 2 \] \[ \frac{1}{4} R^2 \phi^4 \leftrightarrow \frac{1}{4} \lambda \phi^4 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \lambda = R^2 \]

Therefore, the position of the minimum in the Standard Model is:

\[ \phi = \pm \sqrt{\frac{\mu^2}{\lambda}} = \pm \sqrt{\frac{2}{R^2}} = \pm \frac{\sqrt{2}}{R} \]

Thus, the vacuum expectation value is:

\[ v = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{R} \]

Hence, the vacuum expectation value \( v \) is identical in both models.

\[ r(E) = r_v \times \frac{E_v}{E} \]

Remark: Derivation of the Characteristic Velocity Scale \( v_0 \approx 200\,\mathrm{km/s} \) from the Effective Energy of \( \Lambda_{\text{eff}} \)

Based on the results of Section 6 and the vacuum energy density formula in the \( \Lambda \)CDM model, the characteristic velocity scale \( v_0 \) can be derived as follows. At the characteristic scale \(r = R\), i.e., under the condition used to derive \(v_0\), we have \(\Lambda_{\text{eff}} = \Lambda\), and thus the effective energy density is given by:

\[ \rho_\Lambda = \frac{\Lambda c^2}{8\pi G} \approx 10^{-26}\,\mathrm{kg/m^3} \]

Assuming a spherically symmetric region of radius \( R \approx 1000\,\mathrm{kpc} \approx 3.1 \times 10^{22}\,\mathrm{m} \), and noting that \( 10^{22}\,\mathrm{m} \ll 10^{26}\,\mathrm{m} \) (from Section 6), we can treat \( \rho_\Lambda \) as approximately constant within this scale and compute the effective mass:

\[ M_\Lambda = \rho_\Lambda \cdot \frac{4}{3} \pi R^3 \approx 10^{42}\,\mathrm{kg} \]

We now consider the kinetic energy that would correspond to the characteristic velocity scale \( v_0 \approx 200\,\mathrm{km/s} \). Here, the key idea is that we first substitute \( v_0 \approx 200\,\mathrm{km/s} \), converting the effective mass \( M_\Lambda \) entirely into kinetic energy, and then assess what fraction of this energy, \( E \), is actually required. This is the true purpose of the derivation — hence the mathematical steps appear in reverse order.

\[ E = \frac{1}{2} M_\Lambda v_0^2 \quad \Rightarrow \quad v_0 \approx \sqrt{\frac{2E}{M_\Lambda}} \approx 200\,\mathrm{km/s} \]

Thus, the characteristic velocity \( v_0 \approx 200\,\mathrm{km/s} \) naturally emerges from the framework of the \( \Lambda_{\text{eff}} \) model. The corresponding energy \( E \sim 10^{52}\,\mathrm{J} \) is consistent with observationally typical scales.

The total vacuum energy is estimated as \( E_\Lambda = M_\Lambda c^2 \approx 10^{59}\,\mathrm{J} \), which is significantly larger than \( E \sim 10^{52}\,\mathrm{J} \), thereby maintaining energetic consistency.

These results quantitatively support that the scale-dependent term \( \Lambda_{\text{eff}}(r) \), derived from the scalar field, can function as a physically plausible effective repulsive cosmological term.
